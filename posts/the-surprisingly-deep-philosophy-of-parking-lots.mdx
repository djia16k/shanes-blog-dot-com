---
title: "The Surprisingly Deep Philosophy of Parking Lots"
date: "2026-03-07"
excerpt: "I've spent more time thinking about parking lots than any reasonable person should. And I regret nothing."
tags: ["life", "observations"]
---

Nobody talks about parking lots. I mean, *really* talks about them.

We use them constantly. We hate them when they're full. We inexplicably feel a small surge of satisfaction when we find a spot right up front, like we've won something. A trophy, but for cars. The trophy is also a car. You drove the trophy.

But here's what I've been thinking about.

A parking lot is, at its core, a place designed exclusively for absence.

It exists for the gap between where you are and where you're going. It is infrastructure built to hold the thing you use to move so that you can go do the actual thing you wanted to do. The parking lot is never the destination. It is literally the transition between two better things.

And yet we think about it constantly. We circle it. We argue about it. We pay for it. We sometimes sit in it for three minutes finishing a podcast before going inside.

---

I wonder sometimes if most of life is just parking lots.

The stretches between the things. The Monday mornings that aren't quite the weekend anymore but aren't really the week yet. The projects that are 80% done and somehow still feel unfinished. The phases of life where you're between the last thing and the next thing, circling, waiting for a spot to open up.

We treat those transition phases like inconveniences. Like the parking lot — something to get through. But the parking lot is kind of important? It's where you gather yourself before you go in. It's the buffer. The breath.

---

Anyway. I thought about this for forty-five minutes last Tuesday while looking for parking.

Found a great spot, for the record. Right up front.

Felt incredible.

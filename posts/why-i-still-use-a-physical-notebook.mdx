title: "Why I Still Use a Physical Notebook in 2026"
date: "2026-02-20"

---

I have a problem, and the problem is that I have downloaded approximately forty note-taking apps.

Notion. Obsidian. Bear. Apple Notes. Roam Research. Things I built myself and then abandoned. At one point I had a very elaborate system involving tags, backlinks, and a filing structure so detailed I needed notes about how to use my notes.

The notebook has twelve pages of handwritten to-dos and costs four dollars.

The notebook is winning.

---

Here's what I've figured out after years of optimizing my way into worse outcomes:

**The friction is the feature.**

When I type something, it feels provisional. Easy to edit. Easy to delete. Easy to reorganize into a subcategory inside a folder inside a tag cloud that I will check exactly once. Digital notes have infinite malleability, and infinite malleability turns out to be mostly infinite procrastination wearing a productivity costume.

When I write something in a notebook, I'm committing to it a little bit. The pen moves slowly. There's a physical cost to writing something down, which is almost comically small — maybe half a calorie — but apparently that's enough. My brain treats it differently. It treats it like a decision rather than a draft.

I also cannot search my notebook. This sounds like a flaw. It is, in fact, one of the best things about it. If I need to find something and I can't find it by flipping through pages, it either wasn't that important, or I've internalized it well enough that I don't need the note.

---

None of this is to say the apps are bad. They're great for collaboration, for reference material, for documents that need to live somewhere retrievable.

But for thinking? For working things out? Give me a pen that runs out of ink at the worst possible moment and a notebook with a coffee ring on the cover.

That's the system. It's not scalable. It doesn't integrate with anything. I can't share a Notion link to my Tuesday grocery list.

I'm at peace with this.

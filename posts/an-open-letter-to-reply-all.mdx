title: "An Open Letter to Whoever Invented Reply All"
date: "2026-01-15"
excerpt: "You made something powerful. You made something dangerous. You made something that 300 people will use to say 'thanks!' to an email meant for 6."
tags: ["humor", "work"]

Dear Inventor of Reply All,

I hope this email finds you well.

I'm writing on behalf of everyone who has ever been CC'd on a company-wide announcement and then received forty-seven subsequent emails from colleagues across seven time zones saying, in varying degrees of enthusiasm, *"Thanks!"*

We would like a word.

---

Reply All is not inherently a bad idea. Conceptually, it solves a real problem: sometimes everyone on a thread needs to see a response, and the alternative — manually selecting each recipient — is genuinely tedious. I understand the logic. I even respect it.

What I cannot respect is the human capacity for pressing Reply All as an instinct rather than a deliberate choice.

The email says: *"The kitchen on the third floor will be closed for cleaning on Friday."*

Forty-three people Reply All to say: *"Thanks for the heads up!"*

Four people Reply All to say: *"Does this apply to the fourth floor kitchen?"*

Two people Reply All to say: *"Please remove me from this list."*

One person — always one person — Reply All to say: *"I think this was sent to the wrong group?"*

It was not sent to the wrong group. They were CC'd. We are all CC'd. We are all in this together now.

---

I'm not saying we abolish Reply All. I'm saying we should have to pass a brief quiz before using it. Not a long quiz. Three questions, maximum:

1. Does everyone on this thread actually need to see my response?
2. Is my response more substantive than the word "thanks"?
3. Am I confident this is not going to trigger a chain of forty replies across the next three hours?

If you answer "no" to any of these, the button grays out. Just for a moment. Just long enough for you to reconsider.

---

Anyway. Thanks for reading.

Please do not reply all.
